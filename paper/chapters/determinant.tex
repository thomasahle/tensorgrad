
\chapter{Determinant and Inverses}
\section{Determinant}

It's convenient to write the determinant in tensor notation as
\[\mathrm{det}(A)=
\mathbin{
\begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=(a0.base), inner sep=1pt]
   \node (a0) {$A$};
   \node[right=.5em of a0] (dots) {$\cdots$};
   \node[right=.5em of dots] (a1) {$A$};
   \draw (a0.north) -- ++(0,.2) coordinate (a0top);
   \draw (a1.north) -- ++(0,.2) coordinate (a1top);
   \draw (a0.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (a0bot);
   \draw (a1.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (a1bot);
   \draw[line width=2pt] (a0top -| a0.west) -- (a1top -| a1.east);
   \draw[line width=2pt] (a0bot -| a0.west) -- (a1bot -| a1.east);
\end{tikzpicture}}
\]
where the thick bars are the anti-symmetrizers of §\ref{sec:symmetry}.
Recall that, following Penrose's convention, the anti-symmetrizer is \emph{normalized}:
across $n$ edges it denotes the idempotent projection
$P = \frac{1}{n!}\sum_\sigma \mathrm{sign}(\sigma)\,\sigma$,
so the closed diagram above computes $\Tr(P A^{\otimes n} P)$.
When the thick bar appears on its own, as a tensor with $n$ legs,
it equals the Levi-Civita tensor scaled by $1/\sqrt{n!}$:
$\mathbin{
\begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=(a0.base), inner sep=1pt]
   \node (a0) {\tiny $i_1$};
   \node[right=.5em of a0] (a1) {\tiny $i_2$};
   \node[right=.5em of a1] (dots) {$\dots$};
   \node[right=.5em of dots] (a2) {\tiny $i_n$};
   \draw (a0.south) -- ++(0,-.25) coordinate (a0bot);
   \draw (a1.south) -- ++(0,-.25) coordinate (a1bot);
   \draw (a2.south) -- ++(0,-.25) coordinate (a2bot);
   \draw[line width=2pt] (a0bot -| a0.west) -- (a2bot -| a2.east);
\end{tikzpicture}}
=\frac{1}{\sqrt{n!}}\,\varepsilon_{i_1,\dots,i_n}$,
where the raw rank-$n$ Levi-Civita tensor is defined by
\[
   \varepsilon_{i_1, \dots, i_n} =
   \begin{cases}
      \mathrm{sign}(\sigma) & \sigma=(i_1, \dots, i_n) \text{ is a permutation} \\
      0 & \text{otherwise,}
   \end{cases}
\]
so that $P = \frac{1}{n!}\,\varepsilon\otimes\varepsilon$ when the dimension of the edges is $d=n$.
The square root, which follows Cvitanovi\'c's birdtrack normalization~\cite{cvitanovic_birdtracks}, is what makes the two readings agree:
stacking two bar-tensors gives
$\frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n!}}\otimes\frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{n!}} = P$
exactly, so we may evaluate any closed diagram either bar-by-bar or through the projector $P$ and get the same value.
To see that the definition makes sense, let's first consider
\begin{align*}
   \mathrm{det}(I)=
\mathbin{
   \begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=(dots.base), inner sep=1pt]
      \node (a0) {};
      \node[right=.5em of a0] (a1) {};
      \node[right=.5em of a1] (dots) {$\dots$};
      \node[right=.5em of dots] (a2) {};
      \draw (a0.south) -- ++(0,.3) coordinate (a0top);
      \draw (a1.south) -- ++(0,.3) coordinate (a1top);
      \draw (a2.south) -- ++(0,.3) coordinate (a2top);
      \draw (a0.north) -- ++(0,-.3) coordinate (a0bot);
      \draw (a1.north) -- ++(0,-.3) coordinate (a1bot);
      \draw (a2.north) -- ++(0,-.3) coordinate (a2bot);
      \draw[line width=2pt] (a0top -| a0.west) -- (a2top -| a2.east);
      \draw[line width=2pt] (a0bot -| a0.west) -- (a2bot -| a2.east);
   \end{tikzpicture}
}
%&
=
\Tr(P)
%&
=
\frac{1}{n!}
\sum_{i_1, \dots, i_n, j_1, \dots, j_n}
   \varepsilon_{i_1, \dots, i_n}
   \varepsilon_{j_1, \dots, j_n}
   [i=j]
%\\&
=
   \frac{1}{n!}
   \sum_{i_1, \dots, i_n}
   \varepsilon_{i_1, \dots, i_n}^2
%\\&
%=
%   \sum_{i_1, \dots, i_n}
%   [(i_1, \dots, i_n)\text{ is a permutation}]
%\\&
= 1.
\end{align*}
Indeed, $P$ projects onto the space of anti-symmetric order-$n$ tensors,
which for $d=n$ is one-dimensional (spanned by $\varepsilon$),
so its trace is $1$.
In general, since $P$ is idempotent we have
$\Tr(P A^{\otimes n} P) = \Tr(P A^{\otimes n})$,
and expanding $P = \frac{1}{n!}\,\varepsilon\otimes\varepsilon$
recovers the permutation definition of the determinant:
\begin{align*}
   \detstack{$A$}
   &=
   \frac{1}{n!}
   \sum_{i_1, \dots, i_n, j_1, \dots, j_n}
   \varepsilon_{i_1, \dots, i_n}
   \varepsilon_{j_1, \dots, j_n}
   A_{i_1,j_1} \cdots A_{i_n,j_n}
   \\&=
   \frac{1}{n!}
   \sum_{\sigma, \tau}
   \mathrm{sign}(\sigma)
   \mathrm{sign}(\tau)
   A_{\sigma_1,\tau_1} \cdots A_{\sigma_n,\tau_n}
   \\&=
   \frac{1}{n!}
   \sum_{\sigma}
   \mathrm{sign}(\sigma)
   \sum_\tau
   \mathrm{sign}(\tau)
   A_{\sigma_1,\tau_1} \cdots A_{\sigma_n,\tau_n}
   \\&=
   \frac{1}{n!}
   \sum_{\sigma}
   \mathrm{sign}(\sigma)^2
   \mathrm{det}(A)
   \\&=
   \mathrm{det}(A).
\end{align*}
The definition generalizes to Cayley's ``hyper determinants'' by $\dots$.

A curious property is that
\[
   \mathbin{\begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=(a0.base), inner sep=1pt]
      \node (a0) {$A$};
      \node[right=.5em of a0] (dots) {$\cdots$};
      \node[right=.5em of dots] (a1) {$A$};
      \draw (a0.north) -- ++(0,.2) coordinate (a0top);
      \draw (a1.north) -- ++(0,.2) coordinate (a1top);
      \draw (a0.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (a0bot);
      \draw (a1.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (a1bot);
      \draw[line width=2pt] (a0bot -| a0.west) -- (a1bot -| a1.east);
   \end{tikzpicture}}
   =
   \mathbin{\begin{tikzpicture}[baseline=(a0.base), inner sep=1pt]
      \node (a0) {$A$};
      \node[right=.5em of a0] (dots) {$\cdots$};
      \node[right=.5em of dots] (a1) {$A$};
      \draw (a0.north) -- ++(0,.2) coordinate (a0top);
      \draw (a1.north) -- ++(0,.2) coordinate (a1top);
      \draw (a0.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (a0bot);
      \draw (a1.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (a1bot);
      \draw[line width=2pt] (a0top -| a0.west) -- (a1top -| a1.east);
      \draw[line width=2pt] (a0bot -| a0.west) -- (a1bot -| a1.east);
      \node[above=1.5em of a0] (b0) {$\phantom{A}$};
      \node[above=1.5em of dots] (d1) {$\cdots$};
      \node[above=1.5em of a1] (b1) {$\phantom{A}$};
      \draw (b0.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (b0bot);
      \draw (b1.south) -- ++(0,-.2) coordinate (b1bot);
      \draw[line width=2pt] (b0bot -| b0.west) -- (b1bot -| b1.east);
   \end{tikzpicture}}
\]
that is, applying $A^{\otimes n}$ to the bar-tensor just scales it by $\mathrm{det}(A)$.
This is because the bar-tensor spans the one-dimensional image of $P$,
a line which $A^{\otimes n}$ maps to itself.
